      <h2>The <em>ask</em>.</h2>
      <p>QMFS needed bodywork that was <strong>lighter</strong>, <strong>stiffer</strong>, and compliant with IMechE Formula Student structural and scrutineering rules — without blowing the team budget. Previous years ran wet-layup glass composites; the target was to move the full aerodynamic package to carbon fibre via resin infusion while keeping costs defensible to sponsors and judges.</p>
      <p>I led the programme end-to-end across two seasons: material trade studies, FEA sign-off, the CBOM, sponsor negotiation, and the infusion itself — then handed it over to a mentored junior for the third season.</p>

    <div class="reveal delay-1">
      <h2>Wet-layup vs. <em>resin infusion</em>.</h2>
      <p>I ran a formal trade study comparing the two manufacturing routes against the car's performance envelope. Wet-layup was familiar and cheap but inconsistent on fibre-to-resin ratio; infusion demanded tighter process control but promised a lower, repeatable resin fraction.</p>
      <p>Using <strong>CES EduPack</strong> for material ranking and <strong>ANSYS</strong> FEA to stress-check candidate laminate schedules, I specified a 3-ply twill schedule that met the structural margin against downforce loads while hitting the mass target. The report was presented to Formula 1 engineers serving as competition judges.</p>
