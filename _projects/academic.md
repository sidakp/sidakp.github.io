    <div class="reveal delay-1">
      <h2>Camshaft bearings for the <em>Mercedes F1</em> powertrain team.</h2>
      <p>A year-long module in which Mercedes' powertrain engineers briefed the cohort directly on real FIA technical regulations. My task: select a bearing material and geometry that survived the full operating envelope of an F1 camshaft — combustion-driven torsional loads, oil-starved cold starts, thermal cycling — while remaining legal under the current power-unit rules.</p>
      <p>I ran the selection in <strong>CES EduPack</strong>, ranking candidates on fatigue strength-to-density, thermal expansion matching the camshaft alloy, and FIA-permitted material families. The final report was submitted against the same scrutineering criteria a real team would face.</p>

    <div class="mono reveal">§ Study II</div>
    <div class="reveal delay-1">
      <h2>Wind-tunnel testing — <em>subsonic</em> to <em>supersonic</em>.</h2>
      <p>A series of instrumented runs on aerofoils across the speed range — low-subsonic through transonic into supersonic. I processed pressure-tap data and schlieren imagery to characterise lift, drag and shock-formation behaviour, then compared measured coefficients against CFD predictions.</p>
      <p>The study was a useful reality-check on how quickly idealised 2D theory parts company with a physical tunnel section — boundary-layer interference, wall effects and probe intrusion all showed up in the data.</p>
