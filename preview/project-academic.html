§ Study I

Camshaft bearings for the Mercedes F1 powertrain team.

A year-long module in which Mercedes' powertrain engineers briefed the cohort directly on real FIA technical regulations. My task: select a bearing material and geometry that survived the full operating envelope of an F1 camshaft — combustion-driven torsional loads, oil-starved cold starts, thermal cycling — while remaining legal under the current power-unit rules.

I ran the selection in CES EduPack, ranking candidates on fatigue strength-to-density, thermal expansion matching the camshaft alloy, and FIA-permitted material families. The final report was submitted against the same scrutineering criteria a real team would face.

Working inside FIA material clauses forces a different habit of mind: you select not for the best material, but for the best material that is allowed.

§ Study II

Wind-tunnel testing — subsonic to supersonic.

A series of instrumented runs on aerofoils across the speed range — low-subsonic through transonic into supersonic. I processed pressure-tap data and schlieren imagery to characterise lift, drag and shock-formation behaviour, then compared measured coefficients against CFD predictions.

The study was a useful reality-check on how quickly idealised 2D theory parts company with a physical tunnel section — boundary-layer interference, wall effects and probe intrusion all showed up in the data.
